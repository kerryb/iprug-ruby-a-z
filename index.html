notes

This is a lightning talk I gave at IPRUG. It works best in Chrome or Safari.

Navigate using cursor keys, space bar or carriage return. Jump to a specific slide by pressing its letter.

A is for ActiveSupport, the Rails utility gem that provides all the little niceties from blank? to 3.days.from.now.at_beginning_of_day.

Of course you don’t have to only use it in Rails projects, and nowadays it’s quite modular so you can just require the bits you want.

B is for blocks, which are one of Ruby’s most powerful features. Amongst other things, they let you iterate over collections without risking off-by-one errors, and work with files without having to remember to explicitly close them afterwards.

C is for community and culture. I’ve always found the Ruby community to be pretty friendly and helpful, and it’s as much a part of the attraction as the langauge itself.

Of course, there’s also the occasional bit of conflict and controversy to add some excitement!

D is for duck typing, which frees us from a lot of the boilerplate that you need in some languages, and doesn’t lead to anywhere near as many errors as people used to static typing expect.

And, just for Steve, D is also for DHH – love him or hate him, by creating Rails he’s probably done more than most to boost the popularity of Ruby.

E is for Enumerable, the module that gives you nearly 50 collection methods for the price of one – you just need to define each and it does the rest.

Some of Enumerable’s methods, like map, reduce and select are also critical to our next entry …

F is for Functional programming, which for an object-oriented scripting language Ruby supports surprisingly well. You can’t really enforce immutability (there’s freeze, but that only does half a job), but Enumerable and blocks combine to give us what has been described as ‘an acceptable lisp’.

G is for gems – over 33,000 so far and counting. Rubygems might not be the perfect package management system, but it’s not bad (especially if you throw Bundler into the mix)

Despite what tools like hoe would have you believe, creating a gem is incredibly easy, and since GemCutter took over rubygems.org, publishing them is trivial too.

H is for happiness. Matz has said that the primary purpose of Ruby is to “help every programmer in the world to be productive, and to enjoy programming, and to be happy.”

In a similar vein, DHH describes Rails as “optimized for programmer happiness”.

I is for irb (or better still, pry), where you can try things out and see isntant results. When I first started using Ruby this was a breath of fresh air, although it turns out that most modern languages have a similar REPL. I say ‘most’ advisedly, because …

J is for Java, the language rubyists love to hate. It actually turns out to be a rather clunky language running on a very solid platform. Fortunately we have JRuby, which lets us take advantage of one without having to put up with the other.

K is for kitten. Nothing to do with Ruby, but every presentation should have one.

L is for licence. Ruby is released under the permissive MIT licence, and most libraries and tools seem to follow suit, or use some other BSD-like licence.

I don’t know whether it’s just the path of least resistance or whether rubyists care more about sharing their work than forcing others to do the same, but I can’t remember the last time I saw a piece of Ruby code with a GPL licence attached.

M is for magic. Whether it’s metaprogramming, method missing, monkey-patching or mixing in modules, Ruby gives us a lot of tools that let us implement ‘clever’ solutions. To quote Mrs Weasley though, “Just because you’re allowed to use magic now does not mean you have to whip your wands out for everything!”

And, of course, M is also for Matz, who started it all.

N is for nil, and the kind of thing that happens when you let your methods return it.

O is for open source, which seems to be very much part of the Ruby philosophy.

It's also for open classes, but, as with metaprogramming, just because you can add methods to an existing class, it doesn't necessarily mean you should.

P is for pernicketiness and perfectionism (and just a little bit of pedantry). Your code might work, but does that mean it’s finished? Have you remembered the third step in ‘red, green, refactor’ and cleaned up the design so it’ll also be easy to maintain in the future?

Of course, perfectionism needs to be tempered with a healthy dose of pragmatism. Just don’t use it as an excuse for letting your standards slip!

Q is for the Ruby Quiz. Unfortunately even its latest incarnation seems to have dried up, but there are still a couple of hundred entries in the archive, which make excellent practice problems or katas.

R is for Rake, Rack, RVM, Rails and RSpec That’s a fairly random collection of libraries and frameworks which just happen to start with R, but they’re really here as a reminder of how many great tools we have at our disposal.

Of course, no-one can remember them all, so The Ruby Toolbox is a good place to find the right tool for the job.

S is for the Standard Library. Often overlooked, but home to all manner of useful classes and modules.

T is for testing in general, and TDD in particular. Without a compiler to catch silly mistakes, automated tests become even more important (although, of course, TDD is about design rather than testing).

An admission though: I didn't test-drive the Javascript behind this presentation, and it probably shows!

U is for ”Use the source, Luke!” – something that’s much easier to do in an interpreted language like Ruby, which can come in handy for some libraries with less-than-comprehensive documentation.

V is for Vim. Not really Ruby-specific, but enjoying something of a resurgence as the cool rubyist’s editor of choice.

W is for Why the Lucky Stiff, prolific author of Ruby libraries and of course of the unique Poignant Guide. He may have moved on, but you can still see his influence in the lighter side of the Ruby community.

X is for XML. Fortunately there’s nowhere near as much of it about as there is with some other languages, but when we do need to deal with it we have great libraries like Builder and Nokogiri to help.

Y is for young programmers. The mainstream world seems to have finally started to notice the sorry state of computer studies in schools, and Ruby (with a bit of help from projects like Hackety-Hack and KidsRuby) seems as good a language as any for the next generation to start with.

Z is for zero. Some facts about zero in ruby: it’s not false, and dividing a floating point by it gives the constant Infinity, not an error. Most importantly though, zero is the number of slides I have left.

(Except this one)
